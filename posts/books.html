My Bookshelf

01/15/2020 @ 11:43 PM CDT

If you enjoy reading books, then you likely understand how compelling they can be. A book allows a reader the opportunity to experience a reality that may or may not be their own, thereby deepening the reader's perspective on a novel or well-known subject to the reader's own experience. A book, most importantly, tells a story. Authors plan, organize, and craft their own story, someone else's story, a fictional story, or a story about the experiences of groups of people. Below is a list of books I've read (or in two cases, listened to the audio book) whose stories have compelled me toward understanding, changing my perspective, and, in some cases, action. For a select number of the books, I provide a brief introduction with the hope that it may pique your interest.

  • Just Mercy, Bryan Stevenson
  • The Body Keeps the Score, Bessel Van Der Kolk
  • Open, Andre Agassi
  • Shoe Dog, Phil Knight
  • I am Malala, Malala Yousafzai and Christina Lamb
  • Sapiens, Yuval Harari
  • Educated, Tara Westover
  • The Omnivore's Dilemma, Michael Pollan
  • Hillbilly Elegy, J.D. Vance
  • Our America, LeAlan Jones, Lloyd Newman, and David Isay
  • The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People, Stephen Covey
  • Man's Search for Meaning, Viktor Frankl
  • Animal Farm, George Orwell
  • The Handmaid's Tale, Margaret Atwood
  • 1984, George Orwell
  • Steve Jobs, Walter Isaacson
  • Into the Wild, Jon Krakauer
  • One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, Ken Kesey
  • A Random Walk Down Wall Stree, Burton Malkiel
  • When Breath Becomes Air, Paul Kalanithi and Abraham Verghese
  • The China Study, T. Colin Campbell and Thomas M. Campbell II
  • Deep Nutrition, Catherina Shanahan
  • The Inner Game of Tennis, W. Timothy Gallwey
  • Outliers, Malcolm Gladwell
  • The Power of Habit, Charles Duhigg
  • The Catcher in the Rye, J.D. Salinger
  • Make it Stick, Peter C. Brown
  • The Outsiders, S.E. Hinton
